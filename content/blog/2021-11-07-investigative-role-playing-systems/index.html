Introduction

Investigative role-playing games add role-playing systems to a game of deduction.

Sherlock Holmes Consulting Detective, Her Story, and Contradiction are examples of deduction games. Players only progress deduce the meaning of the clues they have.

With investigative role-playing, the players want to role-play a character. It’s perfectly valid to play Sherlock Holmes Consulting Detective while pretending to be other characters, but that approach is limiting. Players want to play characters with skills they don’t possess: casting spells, knowledge of ancient history, or handling weapons. Likewise, my player and I want to experience archetypal mystery encounters like car chases, shootouts, and fist fights. A role-playing system provide rules to use skills and resolve encounters.

I think Gumshoe is a great system for these type of games.

Introduction

There are two components to investigative role-playing: progress is made by logical deduction from a set of clues and players role-play a character with skills and abilities they don’t possess.

In games of pure logical deduction, such as Sherlock Holmes Consulting Detective, Her Story, and Contradiction, progress is only accomplished by following clues. But deduction is only part of a great mystery story; car chases, shootouts, and fist fights all add to the excitment.

By adding a role-playing system onto of a deduction game, the system allows players to role-play characters with skills and abilities they don’t possess, and provides rules to resolve encounters.

In the games I lead the clues and mystery are center stage, not the gameplay encounters. Encounters propel the story in surprising ways and create tension and excitement. But I don’t want the complexity and length of the encounters to overshadow the narrative. Within these constraints I want the rules to maximize the tension.

To demonstrate how the Gumshoe system maximizes tension with simple rules, I’ll analyze it and similar systems using the encounter-model I introduced in my last post.

Why Gumshoe?

Character creation is simple (and getting simpler with modern variants). The rules are designed so the dice rolls are always driven by the player, but lead by the DM so players don’t need to know an exhaustive list of rules. Investigative abilities always work; players can’t miss out on a clue because of a bad roll. But my favorite feature of Gumshoe is the point spends.

Before each roll, a player can choose to spend a number of points from their poll to add to their result. They could add 0 points, making it a coin flip, or 7 guaranteeing a success with the max DC1 of 8. Like spell slots in DnD 5e, the points are a limited resource and have to last for multiple encounters.

The cost is a palpable source of tension in my games. Players agnoize over how much to spend. They worry about how many points to save for future encounters and about a random failure failure occuring despite their spend. This feature really augments the tension set by the narrative.

The system also gives the players a surprising amount of control. During the encounter they must decide: the ability to use, whether to spend points, and the number of points to spend. Then they roll. Each decision simple and fast, but offer enough complexity to feel the weight of the choice. Moreover, the points offer a sense of power, giving them the option for a guaranted success.

By constrast, in D&D 5e mechanical tension is often limited to a single dice roll with no cost or risk. In certain situations, the best a character can do is a basic attack. The phrase “I guess I’ll attack” tells me my players don’t feel in control and don’t feel the tension.

Worse, D&D 5e takes a long time to learn and combat is notoriously slow. Given these reasons, I don’t like to use D&D 5e for role-playing investigations.
